When the territory of the Istmo of Panama gained independence from the Spanish crown at the end of 1821, it voluntarily joined to "La gran Colombia". This country, whose creation was promoted by Simón Bolívar, consisted of present-day Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador and Panama.

Although "La gran Colombia" wasn't a federal state in the modern sense, its territories had autonomy in several aspects, including political and economic.

First attempt to gain independence

Although Panama's union was voluntary, not all Panamanians agreed. The first attempt at separation was in 1826, when Panama didn't accept the constitution that the Bolivarians intended to enact.

The main reason for this attempted separation was the detrimental attitude of the Colombian congress towards the mercantile enterprises of the istmo. For this reason they tried to have Panama protected by the United States and the United Kingdom.

The separatists didn't succeed, but similar movements arose and the result was the emergence of Ecuador and Venezuela as independent nations.

Other attempts of separation

During the following years, the Panamanian territory experienced different administrative models depending on the type of government in Bogotá.

When it was centralist, it became the department of Istmo, without autonomy. But if the federalists ruled, Panama was a state within the federation.

Between 1830 and 1832, there were several attempts at separation. In 1840, the territory became the state of Istmo, on the condition that it was in a federal system, then decided to remain united with the then "Nueva Granada".

  • A deep economic crisis resulting from the failure of the French canal project and the 1000 Days War.
  • Colombia's centralist policy, which was negative and counterproductive for Panama, because all administrative matters were handled from the country's capital.
  • The general neglect of the Istmo, in culture and education.
  • The rejection of the Herran-Hay treaty, which Panamanians saw as a way out of the precarious situation in which they were living due to the lack of jobs.

    • The Herran-Hay treaty was attacked in the Senate by former President Miguel Antonio Caro, Marroquín's main opponent. Caro convinced Congress that Herran-Hay was detrimental to Colombia's interests and sovereignty, which voted overwhelmingly against the treaty on 18 August, causing a worldwide scandal. The directors of the Panama Railway Company bought off several Panamanian leaders and General Esteban Huertas, head of the Colombian Guard. The Marroquín government ignored the rumours of separation to such an extent that on 3 November 1903, Pablo Arosemena sent Marroquín a telegram from Panama City giving a report of normality in the istmo.

    The Thousand Days' War was a civil war fought in Colombia from 17 October 1899 to 21 November 1902, at first between the Liberal Party and the government led by the National Party, and later – after the Conservative Party had ousted the National Party – between the liberals and the conservative government.

    José Agustín Arango, an Isthmian politician, began to work secretly in the preparation of the separatist movement. A clandestine revolutionary junta was formed around him to plan a revolution aimed at consolidating the separation of the Isthmus from Colombian sovereignty, to negotiate directly with the United States the construction of the canal.

    On November 13, 1903, the United States formally recognizes the Republic of Panama, France would do so the following day and before the end of November, fifteen other countries in America, Europe and Asia would do so as well.

    In Colombia, the news of the separation from Panama was not known until December 6 in Bogotá. The reason given to explain the delay was that the submarine cable that made communications between the two regions possible had been damaged in those days.

    It was the Colombian ambassador in Ecuador, who sent the news to the Colombian government, which hid the news for a few days, to prevent possible disturbances that would happen in Bogotá.

    The separation of Panama from Colombia benefited the United States and a rich Panamanian sector.
